# Report on MQ135 Gas Sensor

## Abstract
The MQ135 gas sensor is widely used for monitoring air quality by detecting harmful gases in the environment. It can detect gases such as ammonia (NH₃), carbon dioxide (CO₂), alcohol, benzene, smoke, and nitrogen oxides. The sensor works by measuring the change in resistance of a sensing material when exposed to different gases. This report explains the working principle of the MQ135 gas sensor, its calibration for different gases, and the concept of the Freundlich Adsorption Theorem used to represent its response.

## 1. Introduction
Air pollution monitoring has become important in modern environmental systems. Gas sensors are used to detect harmful gases present in the air.

The MQ135 gas sensor is commonly used in air quality monitoring systems. It is sensitive to gases such as ammonia, nitrogen oxides, alcohol, benzene, and smoke. The sensor converts gas concentration into an electrical signal which can be measured using a microcontroller.

---

## 2. Working Principle
The MQ135 sensor works on the principle of **chemiresistive sensing**.

The sensing element is made of **tin dioxide (SnO₂)** which has low conductivity in clean air. Oxygen molecules in clean air attach to the sensor surface and capture free electrons from the sensing material.

This results in:
- **High resistance**
- **Low conductivity**

When harmful gases such as **NH₃, CO, alcohol, or smoke** are present, they react with the oxygen ions on the sensor surface and release the captured electrons back to the sensing material.

This causes:
- **Decrease in resistance**
- **Increase in conductivity**

The change in resistance is converted into a voltage signal, which can be measured using a microcontroller.

Main components of the sensor include:

- Sensing material (SnO₂)
- Heating element
- Electrodes
- Protective mesh

The heating element maintains a suitable temperature for gas detection.

## 3. Calibration of MQ135 Sensor
Calibration is required to obtain accurate gas concentration values.

The calibration process involves determining **Ro**, which is the sensor resistance in clean air.

### Step 1: Calculate Sensor Resistance (Rs)

Rs can be calculated using the formula:

Rs = ((Vc / Vout) − 1) × RL

Where:

- Vc = Circuit voltage  
- Vout = Output voltage from sensor  
- RL = Load resistance  

### Step 2: Determine Ro

Ro is obtained when the sensor is placed in clean air.

Ro = Rs / 3.6

The value **3.6** is taken from the sensor datasheet for clean air conditions.

### Gas Detection Range

| Gas | Detection Range |
|-----|----------------|
| Ammonia (NH₃) | 10 – 300 ppm |
| Benzene | 10 – 1000 ppm |
| Alcohol | 10 – 300 ppm |
| Smoke | 10 – 1000 ppm |
| Carbon Dioxide (CO₂) | 350 – 10000 ppm |

The ratio **Rs/Ro** is used to estimate the concentration of different gases.

## 4. Freundlich Adsorption Theorem
The MQ135 sensor follows the **Freundlich Adsorption Isotherm**, which describes how gases are adsorbed on a solid surface.

The theorem is expressed as:

x/m = kP^(1/n)

Where:

- x = amount of gas adsorbed  
- m = mass of adsorbent  
- P = pressure or concentration of gas  
- k and n = constants  

Taking logarithm on both sides:

log(x/m) = log k + (1/n) log P

This shows a **linear relationship on a log–log graph**.

## 5. Freundlich Graph
In the MQ135 datasheet, the sensor response is represented using a **log-log graph**.

Graph characteristics:

- **X-axis:** Gas concentration (ppm) in logarithmic scale  
- **Y-axis:** Ratio of sensor resistance (Rs/Ro) in logarithmic scale  

Different curves represent different gases such as:

- NH₃

- Benzene

The graph helps determine the gas concentration by comparing the measured Rs/Ro value.
